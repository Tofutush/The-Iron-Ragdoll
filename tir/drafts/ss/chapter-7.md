Guess what? I had also forgotten you needed a computer to code. And the only computer at home was in Mom's study.

"Can I use your computer, Mom?" I asked over dinner.

"Why? You have your phone already."

"I want to learn programming."

"For class?"

"For a club."

"Programming club?"

"Kinda, yeah."

"Programming is really hard for you," Mom said.

I had expected her to refuse, but not like *this*. "But I haven't even started!"

"Yes, but I know a bit of programming and I know you. All these concepts would be too hard for you to handle. You're already struggling with schoolwork. Don't try to do any extra."

I picked at my buns. I had shredded them to little pieces and soaked them in the porridge, which turned them a more appetizing, but not a lot.

"Besides," Mom continued, "you're only doing this because Sugarcane did, right? You know you don't have to do everything Sugarcane does. Find your own hobbies."

She doesn't *get* it. I *want* to do everything Sugarcane does.

"Sugarcane is a smart kid," Mom said for the billionth time. "Maybe you should just leave her alone in this, so you won't drag her down."

My heart lurched. Mom was… right. If I joined Coding Club with her, she would only be hindered by my slower progress. Besides, now I couldn't even join anyways, because a *certain* parent won't let me use her computer.

After dinner, I went to help Dad with the dishes. And by "help," I meant standing beside him talking. Mom was in her study, doing whatever work she always did. Maybe even doing a bit of coding.

"You don't happen to have a secret computer of your own?" I asked.

"No," he answered curtly.

"You really don't *ever* use a computer? What about your lesson plans? Slides? How do you make those?"

Dad dropped the plate in his hand. It made a clunking noise in the sink, but thankfully didn't break. He hated it whenever I brought up his job, and I never understood why. He was literally just some vocational training teacher.

"I don't need digital devices in my classes. And if I absolutely need a computer one day, I would use your mom's."

Right. Of course *he* got to use Mom's computer.

I gave up and went back to my room to face all my homework. Mom wasn't wrong. I *was* struggling. I hadn't even fully comprehended this topic, and we immediately moved on to the next. According to Mom, normal middle schools taught equations and functions. *We* were learning statistics, because equations and functions wouldn't help with MSS work but statistics would, apparently.

I thought about where I could get a computer to use. Not Sugarcane's, obviously; she needed that, and I was pretty sure she was using her parents' computer too.

I spent all weekend weighing my options, and it suddenly dawned on me. The library! We definitely had a library at our school, though I never went there. There were usually computers at the library, right?

---

"Looks like it's my turn to clean up the classroom this week," Sugarcane frowned at a schedule taped to the wall. Sugarcane's and Bloom's names were next to this week's dates. "You can go home without me."

"No, it's fine, I can wait," I said quickly. "I'll wait in the library."

How the stars had aligned for me. I was wondering when I would have time to study, since I needed to spend every waking moment around Sugarcane. Now I could go through her coding resources while she cleaned up the classroom. I would've offered to help her with the cleanup too; but in fifth grade she insisted on doing it without my help, so I hadn't brought it up since.

I swiped my student card at the library entrance. The door swung open and a gust of cold wind greeted me. Everything seemed to be operating at a full capacity here: the air conditioners, the lights, and the students. The ground was a tranquil light-brown shade of wood. Bookshelves and tables lined up everywhere, with potted plants tucked in every corner.

Colorful cushions lined the seats behind the tables, while students sat on them and studied. It would be really nice to study with Sugarcane here! We would probably make quite a bit of noise though, and the library was so silent even my footsteps made too loud of a sound.

I turned a corner and saw them immediately. Rows and rows of computers, some occupied by students doing all manners of research, but most of them empty. I picked a seat in a corner and turned it on.

For the next half-hour, I read through manuals and tutorials, answered quizzes, and ran my own code. On the last question of the unit, though, I couldn't manage to get the code to work no matter what I tried. I was this close to bashing my head against the screen when a familiar voice whispered in my ear.

"You forgot to save the file. It's been running the old version this whole time."

I turned around to see Sugarcane sitting beside me. How long had she been here? How come I didn't notice? "Sugarcane!" I said, perhaps a little too loudly.

I saved the file and ran the code. The result was correct.

"Yes!" I said under my breath.

"Wanna go home now?" Sugarcane asked.

"Sure," I said as I picked up my backpack.

---

By the time the club meeting took place on Friday, I had gotten to unit three in the online course. The original plan was to do one unit every day, but unit three took me two days. Still, by then both Sugarcane and I felt confident and prepared for whatever the meeting would entail.

I was *not* prepared.

The boy from the clubs fair was the host. The whole time he was talking gibberish while Sugarcane nodded along. His laptop screen was projected for all of us to see while he typed into a hacker-esque interface, black screen with white and shiny green text.

I started to zone out when his voice just became background noise. I stared at Sugarcane, who was taking notes on her phone.

After what seemed like an eternity, it was over. Sugarcane headed to the front of the room to sign up and join the club while I waited in my seat.

"You're not joining?" She asked.

"No," I sighed. "I hope you have fun, though."

"Thanks," she said. "Will you continue to learn programming, though?"

I wasn't sure. "Maybe." I hated that the *one* time I didn't notice Sugarcane next to me was when I got enthralled in a coding problem. Besides, Mom was right. I would only drag her down.

"I'm glad," Sugarcane smiled. "Also, isn't the wilderness survival training tomorrow?"

"Right! Oh my," I said.

"There goes the weekend!" Sugarcane threw her head back.

But at least we would be together. Unless they separate everyone. Would they?

"I have a bad feeling," I said, "that this would be normal now. This weekend is wilderness survival. The next weekend, Moreland survival."

"I bet Moreland survival wouldn't even be that different from wilderness survival. And the week after that, MSS office field trip. Immersive getting-yelled-at-by-your-boss experience."

We laughed the whole way home, parting at Sugarcane's.

At home, Mom was already packing up a bag for the weekend. "I prepared a lot of freshly-steamed buns for you," she said.

"Freshly-steamed? What about the old ones?"

"They were getting moldy so I threw a lot of them away."

"Why did you make this many in the first place?"

"Ehh," Mom shrugged awkwardly. "I like making them. Helps me relax."

Along with the buns, she also packed a week's worth of clothes and a sleeping bag she bought yesterday. The P.E. teacher had said tents would be provided, and I thought that would be enough, but Mom insisted that tents were just a thin piece of fabric and I still needed a bag for a good-night's sleep.

"I also prepared matches, lighters, mosquito sprays, flashlights and batteries —"

"Mom! Let me do this myself!" I said. I knew she wouldn't let me, and I was secretly quite grateful she got all of this ready for me, but still. How was I going to learn anything if she did all of it for me?

"Sure, you can look through here. I'll fetch the pocket knife in my room."

I looked in the bag, but couldn't really think of anything Mom hadn't already put there, so I just waited until she returned with the pocket knife. The bag was heavy, stuffed full of excesses. Who needed three flashlights and two packs of replacement batteries for one night? But I didn't dare remove anything, in case Mom scolded me and put them right back.

She wedged the pocket knife into the cupholder, next to my water bottle. I slung the bag across my shoulders. It was heavy but bearable, though I worried that it would get heavier as I got more tired bearing it. If it came to that, I could just drop one of the two hundred cans of mosquito spray.

The next morning, I met Sugarcane at our usual spot in front of her apartment. She only had a light bag with some snacks, and her eyes popped when she saw my gigantic abomination.

"Did I bring too little or did you bring too much?" Her gaze toggled between her bag and mine.

"My mom." I shook my head.

"Let me carry that," she frowned.

"No way!" I ducked under her hand and made a run for it, which proved to be a bad idea as I nearly fell over under the weight. Sugarcane caught me.

"That does not look good," she said. "You sure you can manage that?"

"I'm sure," I said. We made it to school with no problems.

Thunder was near the gate. "To the clearing on the right, there's a bus," he said and yawned.

"Thanks," Sugarcane said and headed towards the clearing facing our classrooms, and sure enough, the P.E. teacher stood in front of a bus.

"Get on," she ushered us inside. It was half-filled, so it was easy to find two seats together.

The bus slowly filled, and Sugarcane invited Cosmo and Sunny to sit in the two seats in front of us. To our surprise, Jewel also boarded the bus.

"Jewel?" Sunny asked. "What are you doing here?"

"I missed this last year," she said. "So I have to make it up."

She sat behind us, and the P.E. teacher got on herself. "Class five," she announced. "There may be some eighth and ninth graders here who need to redo this assignment. Don't be like them."

Thunder did attendance, and after confirming everyone was here, we were on our way.

It felt like an eternity, and the smell of bus seats made me sick. I drifted off to sleep with my heavy bag in my laps.

Sugarcane shook me awake as the bus pulled to a stop. "We're here."

I reluctantly stood up, stretching my numb limbs and put on my backpack. I checked the time. It was half past nine. We had been traveling for two hours??

We seemed to be in the middle of nowhere. The bus stood to the side of a dirt path leading to a nondescript gray block of a building standing in the center of attention, with equally gray and imposing walls extending from its sides all the way to the horizons. All around us were patchy, yellowed grass and weeds, with a few trees here and there. If we had all been kidnapped, I wouldn't have imagined anything different.

"Get in a line," the teacher ordered. She walked up to the building, pressed her finger to a pad, and the doors slid open, groaning all the way.

We filed into the building. The door beeped for each person passing through. A metal detector?

The entire building seemed to just be an antechamber, with the only other door being on the opposite wall. There was a single light flickering on the ceiling. It looked larger from the outside. Were there any secret rooms? Yet I couldn't find a single other door.

The teacher produced a box. "Put your phones in here." The box was passed around as students dropped their phones inside. I parted with mine, whispering to it that we would reunite soon. Some people didn't have phones in the first place.

The teacher looked at her watch. "You'll start at ten o'clock sharp," she said. "We will come collect everyone at four P.M. tomorrow. During this time, you must reach the mountain in the far side at least once, and put your name on the sheet in the cave."

"What exactly is behind the door?" A student asked.

"You'll find out. Don't worry. If you die, your family will be duly compensated."

That was not reassuring at all.

"And we hadn't had a major incident since the Unfortunate Mass Bloodshed of 2034."

Even less reassuring.

"You are free to form groups, but I will be breaking up groups larger than five people."

Make it two! Make it *two*!

"Each group send a person up here to get a tent."

Cosmo and Sunny had already settled next to me and Sugarcane. To my surprise, Jewel was also edging closer to us. "I'll go get our tent!" She volunteered.

An alarm bell rung. It was the teacher's own phone.

"It's time." She said. She opened the other door and ushered us out.

My jaws dropped. There was a *jungle* behind this. Trees were everywhere, and the entire ground was covered in shrubs.

"Anywhere within fifty meters of the wall is off-limits. Now scram!"
