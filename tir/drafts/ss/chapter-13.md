Guess what? I had also forgotten you needed a computer to code. And the only computer at home was in Mom's study.

"Can I use your computer, Mom?" I asked over dinner.

"Why? You have your phone already."

"I want to learn programming."

"For class?"

"For a club."

"Programming club?"

"Kinda, yeah."

"Programming is really hard for you," Mom said.

I had expected her to refuse, but not like *this*. "But I haven't even started!"

"Yes, but I know a bit of programming and I know you. All these concepts would be too hard for you to handle. You're already struggling with schoolwork. Don't try to do any extra."

I picked at my buns. I had shredded them to little pieces and soaked them in the porridge, which turned them a little more appetizing, but not a lot.

"Besides," Mom continued, "you're only doing this because Sugarcane did, right? You know you don't have to do everything Sugarcane does. Find your own hobbies."

She doesn't *get* it. I *want* to do everything Sugarcane does.

"Sugarcane is a smart kid," Mom said for the billionth time. "Maybe you should just leave her alone in this, so you won't drag her down."

My heart lurched. Mom was… right. If I joined Coding Club with her, she would only be hindered by my slower progress. Besides, now I couldn't even join anyways, because a *certain* parent won't let me use her computer.

After dinner, I went to help Dad with the dishes. And by "help," I meant standing beside him talking. Mom was in her study, doing whatever work she always did. Maybe even doing a bit of coding.

"You don't happen to have a secret computer of your own?" I asked.

"No," he answered curtly. Or rather, he answered normally, since curtness was his norm.

"You really don't *ever* use a computer? What about your lesson plans? Slides? How do you make those?"

Dad dropped the plate in his hand. It made a clunking noise in the sink, but thankfully didn't break. He hated it whenever I brought up his job, and I never understood why. He was literally just some vocational training teacher.

"I don't need digital devices in my classes. And if I absolutely need a computer one day, I would use your mom's."

Right. Of course *he* got to use Mom's computer.

I gave up and went back to my room to face all my homework. Mom wasn't wrong. I *was* struggling. I hadn't even fully comprehended this topic, and we immediately moved on to the next. According to Mom, normal middle schools taught equations and functions. *We* were learning statistics, because equations and functions wouldn't help with MSS work but statistics would, apparently.

I thought about where I could get a computer to use. Not Sugarcane's, obviously; she needed that, and I was pretty sure she was using her parents' computer too.

I spent all weekend weighing my options, and it suddenly dawned on me. The library! We definitely had a library at our school, though I never went there. There were usually computers at the library, right?

---

"Looks like it's my turn to clean up the classroom this week," Sugarcane frowned at a schedule taped to the wall. Sugarcane's and Bloom's names were next to this week's dates. "You can go home without me."

"No, it's fine, I can wait," I said quickly. "I'll wait in the library."

How convenient. I was wondering when I would have time to study, since I needed to spend every waking moment around Sugarcane. Now I could go through her coding resources while she cleaned up the classroom. I would've offered to help her with the cleanup too; but in fifth grade she insisted on doing it without my help, so I hadn't brought it up since.

I swiped my student card at the library entrance. The door swung open and a gust of cold wind greeted me. Everything seemed to be operating at a full capacity here: the air conditioners, the lights, and the students. The ground was a tranquil light-brown shade of wood. Bookshelves and tables lined up everywhere, with potted plants tucked in every corner.

Colorful cushions lined the seats behind the tables, while students sat on them and studied. It would be really nice to study with Sugarcane here! We would probably make quite a bit of noise though, and the library was so silent even my footsteps made too loud of a sound.

I turned a corner and saw them immediately. Rows and rows of computers, some occupied by students doing all manners of research, but most of them empty. I picked a seat in a corner and turned it on.

For the next half-hour, I read through manuals and tutorials, answered quizzes, and ran my own code. On the last question of the unit, though, I couldn't manage to get the code to work no matter what I tried. I was this close to bashing my head against the screen when a familiar voice whispered in my ear.

"You forgot to save the file. It's been running the old version this whole time."

I nearly jumped out of my seat, the instinctive part of my brain thinking it was Rose taunting me. But as soon as the surprise wore off, I knew I recognized the voice. I would never *not* recognize the voice.

"Sugarcane!" I said, perhaps a little too loudly. How long had she been here? How come I didn't notice?

I saved the file and ran the code. The result was correct.

"Yes!" I said under my breath.

"Wanna go home now?" Sugarcane asked.

"Sure," I said as I picked up my backpack.

By the time the club meeting took place on Friday, I had gotten to unit three in the online course. The original plan was to do one unit every day, but unit three took me two days. Still, by then both Sugarcane and I felt confident and prepared for whatever the meeting would entail.

I was *not* prepared.

The boy from the clubs fair was the host. The whole time he was talking gibberish while Sugarcane nodded along. His laptop screen was projected for all of us to see while he typed into a hacker-esque interface, black screen with white and shiny green text.

Most of the people here had their own laptops, where they typed into similar black screens. It was like some hacker's enclave. The people without laptops were mostly middle-schoolers, either staring intently at the projector screen, or nodding off, like me.

The host's voice just became background noise. I stared at Sugarcane, who was taking notes on her phone.

After what seemed like an eternity, it was over. Sugarcane headed to the front of the room to sign up and join the club while I waited in my seat.

"You're not joining?" She asked.

"No," I sighed. "I hope you have fun, though."

"Thanks," she said. "Will you continue to learn programming, though?"

I wasn't sure. "Maybe." Mom was right. I would only drag her down.

"I'm glad," Sugarcane smiled. "Also, isn't the wilderness survival training tomorrow?"

"Right! Oh my," I said.

"There goes the weekend!" Sugarcane threw her head back.

But at least we would be together. Unless they separate everyone. Would they?

"I have a bad feeling," I said, "that this would be normal now. This weekend is wilderness survival. The next weekend, Moreland survival."

"I bet Moreland survival wouldn't even be that different from wilderness survival. And the week after that, MSS office field trip. Immersive getting-yelled-at-by-your-boss experience."

We laughed the whole way home, parting at Sugarcane's.
